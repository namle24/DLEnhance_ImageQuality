\begin{abstract}

This paper presents a curriculum-guided Siamese Real-ESRGAN framework for practical image restoration under different degradation severities. The proposed method extends high-order degradation synthesis with probabilistic operation selection, realistic compression ranges, camera-specific noise simulation, and offline multi-level low-quality image generation. During training, two degraded versions of the same high-resolution image are used as a paired input: the milder version provides restoration guidance, while the harder version encourages robustness to stronger artifacts. A shared-weight generator processes both inputs, enabling self-guided learning without introducing an additional teacher network or increasing inference-time complexity. A mild-to-hard curriculum further stabilizes training by gradually increasing the degradation difficulty. Experiments on standard and real-world benchmarks show that the proposed method improves perceptual quality and produces cleaner textures, sharper structures, and fewer visual artifacts compared with the baseline Real-ESRGAN model.

\keywords{Image Super-Resolution \and Siamese Network \and Knowledge Distillation \and Curriculum Learning \and Real-ESRGAN}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Single-image super-resolution (SR) aims to recover a high-resolution image from a degraded low-resolution input. It plays an important role in various domains including digital photography, surveillance, remote sensing, medical imaging, and visual content enhancement, where image details are often lost due to limited sensor resolution, compression, noise, blur, or transmission constraints. Although recent deep learning-based SR methods have achieved remarkable results, many of them are trained under simplified synthetic degradations, which do not fully represent real-world image degradation.

In practical scenarios, low-quality images usually contain a complex mixture of unknown degradations, including optical blur, sensor noise, JPEG compression, resizing artifacts, and camera post-processing effects. This gap between synthetic training data and real-world inputs often causes SR models to generate over-smoothed textures, amplified noise, ringing artifacts, or visually unrealistic details. To address this issue, real-world SR methods such as ESRGAN~\cite{wang2018esrgan}, BSRGAN~\cite{zhang2021designing}, and Real-ESRGAN~\cite{wang2021realesrgan} introduce more realistic degradation models and perceptual learning objectives. Among them, Real-ESRGAN has become a strong baseline by using a high-order degradation pipeline to simulate complex real-world artifacts.

However, learning from real-world degradations remains challenging because image quality may vary from mildly degraded to severely corrupted. Training directly on strong degradations can make optimization unstable and may lead to unreliable texture generation, while training mainly on mild degradations may reduce robustness on difficult inputs. Inspired by curriculum learning~\cite{Bengio09}, a natural strategy is to expose the model to multiple degradation levels in a progressive manner, allowing it to first learn from easier cases where structural information is still preserved and then gradually adapt to harder cases with stronger artifacts. In this work, we adopt this mild-to-hard training strategy by constructing paired degradation levels from the same high-resolution image, so that easier degradation cases can support the learning of more difficult restoration scenarios.

The main contributions of this paper are two-fold. First, we propose a shared-weight Siamese Real-ESRGAN training scheme that improves restoration guidance without increasing inference-time complexity. Second, we design a mild-to-hard curriculum scheme using progressive degradation pairs and an enhanced high-order degradation pipeline to stabilize learning under increasingly severe degradations.


\section{Related Work}
This section reviews image super-resolution methods most related to real-world degradation modeling, knowledge distillation, and curriculum learning.
Deep learning has improved image super-resolution by learning the mapping from low-quality to high-quality images. SRCNN~\cite{dong2014learning} showed the effectiveness of end-to-end CNN learning, while EDSR~\cite{lim2017enhanced} and RDN~\cite{zhang2018residualdensenetworkimage} improved reconstruction through deeper residual and dense architectures. ESRGAN~\cite{wang2018esrgan} introduced adversarial and perceptual losses for sharper textures, and SwinIR~\cite{liang2021swinir} further strengthened restoration with Transformer blocks. However, many of these methods rely on simplified synthetic degradations, which limits robustness when test images contain mixed real-world artifacts. Real-world SR methods therefore focus on more practical degradation synthesis. BSRGAN~\cite{zhang2021designing} combines blur, downsampling, noise, and JPEG compression in a randomized degradation pipeline. Real-ESRGAN~\cite{wang2021realesrgan} further uses a high-order degradation process and has become a strong baseline for blind real-world SR. Our work follows this direction, but organizes the generated samples into explicit degradation levels so that training can exploit mild-to-severe relationships instead of treating all degraded inputs as one mixed distribution.

Knowledge distillation transfers information from a Teacher model to a Student model~\cite{hinton2015distilling}. In SR, distillation is often used for model compression or feature-level guidance. Different from methods with separate Teacher and Student networks, our framework uses two degraded versions of the same image and one shared generator. The milder branch provides output-level and feature-level guidance for the harder branch without adding inference-time parameters. Our training strategy is also related to curriculum learning~\cite{Bengio09}, where models are exposed to easier samples before harder ones. Here, difficulty is defined by degradation strength. The model first learns from mild degradation pairs and then moves to stronger pairs, which helps stabilize optimization under severe real-world degradations.

\subsection{Multi-Level High-Order Degradation}

Our degradation module follows the high-order pipeline of Real-ESRGAN and the refined setting in~\cite{11309534}. We keep the two-stage degradation structure, but generate four explicit quality levels by changing noise, compression, and blur parameters. Camera-specific noise, including chromatic aberration and sensor shot/read noise, is also included to better approximate real acquisition.
For an HR image $I_{HQ}$, the generated LQ image at quality level $q$ is defined as
\[
I_{LQ}^{(q)} = \mathcal{D}_{q}(I_{HQ})
= \Pi\big(R_{0.25}, B_{\Theta_q}, N_{\Theta_q}, C, J_{\Theta_q}\big)(I_{HQ}),
\]
where $R$, $B$, $N$, $J$, and $C$ denote resizing, blur, noise, JPEG compression, and camera-specific noise, respectively. The resize factor is fixed to $0.25$ for $\times4$ SR. The level $q\in\{90,80,70,60\}$ is a quality index: $q=100$ denotes the original GT image, larger $q$ means milder degradation, and lower $q$ means stronger degradation. In the two-stage process, resizing is applied first, while blur, noise, camera noise, and JPEG compression are sampled probabilistically and then repeated in the second pass.

Each quality level changes the parameter set
\[
\Theta_q=(\Sigma_q,Q_q,p^B_q),
\]
where $\Sigma_q$ is the Gaussian-noise sigma range, $Q_q$ is the JPEG quality range, and $p^B_q$ is the blur probability:
\[
\begin{array}{c|c|c|c}
q & \Sigma_q & Q_q & p^B_q \\
\hline
90 & [1,10] & [75,95] & 0.4\\
80 & [5,15] & [60,85] & 0.6\\
70 & [10,20] & [45,70] & 0.7\\
60 & [15,25] & [30,55] & 0.8
\end{array}
\]
The remaining probabilities are shared across levels: $p^{resize}=1.0$, $p^{noise}=0.9$, $p^{JPEG}=0.9$, and $p^{camera}=0.7$. Thus, 90\% samples preserve more structure, while 60\% samples contain stronger noise, heavier compression, and more frequent blur.

For $\times4$ SR, GT images are cropped into $480\times480$ patches with 50\% overlap, producing $120\times120$ LQ counterparts. Each patch is pre-degraded into four quality levels. This makes the training pairs content-aligned and reproducible: 90/80, 80/70, and 70/60 always come from the same HR patch.

\subsection{Siamese Training with Shared Weights}
Fig.~\ref{fig:siamese_architecture} shows the shared-weight Teacher--Student training design. The generator is the Real-ESRGAN RRDBNet backbone~\cite{wang2018esrgan,wang2021realesrgan} with 23 RRDB blocks and 64 feature channels. Each mini-batch contains two degraded versions of the same GT image. The Teacher branch processes the milder input under \texttt{torch.no\_grad()} and produces detached targets $T_{HQ}$ and $T_{feat}$. The Student branch processes the harder input and produces $S_{HQ}$ and $S_{feat}$. Both branches use the same generator weights; only the Student branch receives gradients.

The training objective combines reconstruction, perceptual, adversarial, and distillation losses:
\[
L_{total}=w_{pix}L_{pix}+w_{per}L_{per}+w_{GAN}L_{GAN}
\;+\lambda^{out}_{kd}L^{out}_{kd}+\lambda^{feat}_{kd}L^{feat}_{kd}.
\]
$L_{pix}$ is L1 loss to GT, $L_{per}$ is VGG19 perceptual loss~\cite{simonyan2014very,johnson2016perceptual}, and $L_{GAN}$ uses a spectral-normalized U-Net discriminator. $L^{out}_{kd}$ aligns the restored images, and $L^{feat}_{kd}$ aligns intermediate features between the Student branch and the detached Teacher targets. This provides guidance from a milder degradation level without storing or deploying a second model.

\subsection{Curriculum Schedule}

The curriculum schedule controls which quality pair is used during training. Following curriculum learning~\cite{Bengio09}, we define difficulty by degradation strength: lower quality levels are harder because they contain stronger blur, noise, compression, and camera artifacts. Instead of mixing all levels from the beginning, the model is trained from mild pairs to severe pairs. Training is divided into three phases. \emph{Phase~1} uses the 90\%/80\% pair, where both inputs are mildly degraded and most structural information is preserved. \emph{Phase~2} uses the 80\%/70\% pair, increasing the degradation severity and encouraging recovery of more corrupted textures and edges. \emph{Phase~3} uses the 70\%/60\% pair, where the harder input contains the strongest artifacts.

In each phase, the first element of the pair is the milder guidance input, and the second element is the harder restoration input. Since both images are generated from the same HR patch, they remain content-aligned and can support output-level and feature-level KD. This progressive schedule lets the model adapt to difficult degradations without starting directly from the hardest 70\%/60\% pair.


\section{Experiments}
\subsection{Experimental Setup}

Training uses high-resolution images from DF2K and Flickr2K~\cite{Timofte2017NTIRE2C}, Unsplash~\cite{unsplash_dataset}, and RealSR~\cite{realsr}. Only HR images are retained; all LQ images are generated by our degradation pipeline. 
%Unlike bicubic pairs, our data combines multi-stage blur, resizing, noise, camera artifacts, compression, probabilistic operations, and multiple severity levels. 
This better approximates real acquisition but makes pixel-faithful reconstruction harder. Evaluation uses Set5~\cite{set5}, Set14~\cite{set14}, RealSR Canon/Nikon~\cite{realsr}, BSD100~\cite{bsd100}, and Urban100\cite{urban100}.


All models are trained with Adam using an initial learning rate of $1\times10^{-4}$ for 800k iterations, including a 10k-iteration warm-up. MultiStepLR decays the learning rate at 200k, 400k, 500k, and 700k iterations. Loss weights are set to $w_{pix}=2.0$, $w_{GAN}=0.5$, and $\lambda^{out}{kd}=\lambda^{feat}{kd}=0.15$. The 16.7M-parameter generator uses a single inference pass, so the proposed training scheme adds no inference-time model size or runtime complexity. Experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU.

Table~\ref{tab:quantitative_results} compares variants within the Real-ESRGAN family. The first three rows are non-curriculum references: the released Real-ESRGAN with \textit{Original online} degradation, the same backbone with our \textit{Multi-level} degradation, and the previous student model trained on multi-level data. The last three rows are our Siamese curriculum phases, where the degradation setting specifies the paired levels used for Teacher--Student training.

The restoration metrics show that our framework improves perceptual restoration quality compared with existing Real-ESRGAN-based variants. Compared with the released Real-ESRGAN, Phase~2 reduces NIQE from 7.02 to 5.71 on RealSR Canon, from 6.69 to 5.69 on RealSR Nikon, and from 4.35 to 3.82 on Urban100. Phase~3 further gives the best NIQE on Set14 and RealSR Canon/Nikon. These gains are achieved without a large runtime increase: the Siamese branches are used only during training, while inference still uses a single Real-ESRGAN generator. This indicates cleaner textures, sharper structures, and fewer visual artifacts, although some PSNR/SSIM scores decrease because the model is optimized more toward perceptual quality.

The phase results reveal that curriculum training should stop at an appropriate difficulty level instead of always moving to harder phases. Phase~1 starts from the mild 90\%/80\% pair and preserves structure well, while Phase~2 gives the best overall perceptual balance, especially on Urban100 and RealSR Canon. Phase~3 uses the hardest 70\%/60\% pair and further improves NIQE on Set14 and RealSR Canon/Nikon, but with lower PSNR/SSIM, which follows the perception-distortion trade-off~\cite{blau2018perception}. This supports selecting the phase that best matches the target quality trade-off.

As a short diagnostic, we also trained a w/o curriculum variant directly on the hardest 70\%/60\% pair for 120k iterations. It reached 22.13 dB PSNR and 0.665 SSIM on Set5, with NIQE scores of 6.26 on RealSR Canon, 6.22 on RealSR Nikon, and 4.14 on Urban100. Although this run uses a smaller budget than the main Table~\ref{tab:quantitative_results} models, it suggests that starting directly from the hardest pair is less favorable than using a gradual curriculum.

\section{Conclusion}
Pairing two degradation severities of the same image through a shared-weight Siamese training strategy consistently improves perceptual quality without increasing inference cost. The proposed curriculum achieves the best NIQE of 3.82 on Urban100 and reduces NIQE on RealSR\_V3 Canon from 7.02 to 5.71 (18.7\%) compared with the original Real-ESRGAN. These results suggest that progressively increasing degradation difficulty is more effective than directly training on the most challenging samples. Future work will investigate automatic curriculum scheduling and lightweight generative refinement to further improve restoration under severe real-world degradations.
